Institutional Factors

The institutional factors we considered while developing our policy.

Economy

Vietnam's GDP per capita growth has been among the fastest in the world over the last 30 years. Accelerating this growth are three main sectors of the Vietnamese economic system: oil and gas, technology, and manufacturing. As the Vietnamese market continues to steadily expand into the 21st Century, the United States must be at the forefront of the economic progress.

Labor

Human rights abuses, based primarily upon the exploitation of workers, are manifested through the mistreatment of the Vietnamese labor force. Without the ability to assemble or protest, labor costs in Vietnam are particularly low even for Southeast Asian countries. As of 2013, the average monthly wage in Vietnam was just $197, compared to $613 for China. Improvement of labor conditions is also a win for the American markets.

Government

Currently in Vietnam, the Communist Party has complete political control. In direct violation of Article 25 of the International Covenant on Civil Rights, which Vietnam signed in 1982, the Communist Party suppresses opposition by preventing official organization or the contesting of governmental elections. Such a lack of political or religious freedom adds to the sore state of human rights in Vietnam.

Our Policy

We propose that through a bilateral relationship with Vietnam centered on economic cooperation, the United States can lend a hand to improving Vietnam’s poor labor conditions while stimulating the economies of both nations. By implementing subsidies and reducing import tariffs, American companies will be adequately compensated to gradually shift away from exploitative labor practices that currently guarantee them their high profits. In fact, American corporations are projected to experience slight profit growth from trade with Vietnam once the policy is fully implemented.
